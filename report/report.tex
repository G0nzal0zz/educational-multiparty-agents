\section{Questionnaire}
\label{app:questionnaire}

The full questionnaire administered to participants after each session is
reproduced below. It consists of four parts: prior knowledge, learning
experience, a knowledge assessment, and qualitative feedback.

\subsection*{Part 1: Prior Knowledge}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Session Type} (single choice, required):
    \begin{itemize}
      \item Teacher + AI peer student
      \item Teacher only
    \end{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Prior Knowledge} (required): \emph{Before this session, how
    familiar were you with Simpson's Paradox?} \\
    (1 = Never heard of it $\rightarrow$ 5 = Very familiar)
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Part 2: Learning Experience}

All items below are rated on a 5-point Likert scale
(1 = Strongly disagree $\rightarrow$ 5 = Strongly agree).

\begin{enumerate}
  \setcounter{enumi}{2}
  \item The content delivered by the AI teacher was clear and easy to follow.
  \item I felt actively engaged during the session.
  \item The pace of the spoken dialogue felt natural and conducive to
    learning.
  \item I feel more confident in my understanding of the topic after this
    session.
  \item \emph{(Teacher + AI student group only)} The AI peer student's
    questions helped me better understand the topic.
  \item \emph{(Teacher + AI student group only)} The presence of the AI peer
    student made the session feel more like a real classroom.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Part 3: Knowledge Assessment}

\begin{enumerate}
  \setcounter{enumi}{8}
  \item \textbf{What is Simpson's Paradox?}
    \begin{enumerate}
      \item A formula used to calculate the probability of an event based
        on conditional probabilities.
      \item A statistical test used to determine the significance of an
        observed correlation.
      \item A graphical representation of data where the trend among
        variables is plotted.
      \item A phenomenon where a trend observed within multiple groups
        reverses when the groups are combined.
    \end{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Which of the following is an example of Simpson's Paradox?}
    \begin{enumerate}
      \item A study showing that eating more vegetables leads to improved
        health outcomes.
      \item The decline in pirates causing an increase in global
        temperatures.
      \item The increase in the price of gasoline leading to an increase
        in public transportation usage.
      \item UC Berkeley graduate admissions showing gender bias when
        aggregated, but not when viewed by department.
    \end{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Which of the following best describes the role of
    confounding variables in Simpson's Paradox?}
    \begin{enumerate}
      \item They are variables that directly cause the reversal of trends.
      \item They are external factors that explain why an observed trend
        reverses when data is aggregated.
      \item They represent the dependent variables in the study.
      \item They are used to calculate the probability of Simpson's
        Paradox occurring.
    \end{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{What is a common misconception about Simpson's Paradox?}
    \begin{enumerate}
      \item That it only occurs in healthcare and medicine.
      \item That it can be resolved simply by collecting more data.
      \item That it is a statistical error correctable with mathematical
        formulas.
      \item That it is a type of data visualisation graph.
    \end{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{In which situation is recognising Simpson's Paradox most
    critical?}
    \begin{enumerate}
      \item While calculating the mean and standard deviation of a dataset.
      \item When choosing the best graphical representation for data.
      \item When determining budget allocation for a marketing campaign.
      \item During the analysis of clinical trial data to ensure correct
        interpretation of treatment effectiveness.
    \end{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{What is the first step in identifying Simpson's Paradox
    in a dataset?}
    \begin{enumerate}
      \item Examining trends within individual subgroups before analysing
        the aggregated data.
      \item Applying complex mathematical formulas to the dataset.
      \item Running statistical tests on the aggregated data immediately.
      \item Collecting more data to increase the sample size.
    \end{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{A researcher finds that a new drug appears effective
    overall in a clinical trial, but is ineffective in every individual
    age group tested. What is the most likely explanation?}
    \begin{enumerate}
      \item The drug's effectiveness varies too much to be measured
        accurately.
      \item The age groups were not large enough to draw conclusions.
      \item The overall result is driven by unequal group sizes.
      \item The trial was not randomised, making all results invalid.
    \end{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Which of the two images represents Simpson's Paradox?}
    (Participants were shown two scatter plots with subgroup regression
    lines and asked to choose between Option~1 and Option~2.)
    \begin{enumerate}
      \item Option 1
      \item Option 2
    \end{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Is this an example of Simpson's Paradox?} (Participants
    were shown the classic kidney-stone treatment table: Treatment~A
    achieves 93\% (81/87) on small stones and 73\% (192/263) on large
    stones; Treatment~B achieves 87\% (234/270) on small stones and 69\%
    (55/80) on large stones; aggregated, Treatment~A is 78\% (273/350)
    and Treatment~B is 83\% (289/350).)
    \begin{enumerate}
      \item No, because there is no trend reversal.
      \item Yes, because the aggregated values (both stones) of success
        percentage in Treatment~B is higher than A, but in the subgroups
        A is better than B.
      \item Yes, because the probability of success is determined by the
        treatment types.
      \item No, because the aggregated values of success percentage in
        Treatment~B is not higher than A.
    \end{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Please rate your overall level of confidence in your
    answers:} 5-point emoji scale
    (1 = Not confident $\rightarrow$ 5 = Very confident).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Part 4: Qualitative Feedback}

Free-text (open-ended) responses.

\begin{enumerate}
  \setcounter{enumi}{18}
  \item What was your overall impression of the spoken interaction?
  \item Were there any moments where the conversation felt unnatural or
    difficult to follow? If so, describe them.
  \item \emph{(Teacher + AI student group only)} During the session, did
    you notice or suspect that the peer student was an AI rather than a
    real person? Did this affect your experience?
  \item Do you have any other comments or suggestions?
  \item \textbf{Overall experience?} 5-point heart-rating scale
    (1 = Poor $\rightarrow$ 5 = Excellent).
\end{enumerate}
